My theory of mind story

A story from elementary school

The ability to attribute beliefs to other people is known as having a “theory of mind.” (Gallagher and Frith 2003). Researchers have stated that children as young as three have a “primitive” theory of mind (Fodor 1992), but that this “commonsense” thinking changes during “subsequent development” from ages three to six (Wellman 1992) and that only by age five or so are children able to explain why another person came to a false belief (Frith and Frith 2005). It has been disputed whether chimpanzees have a “rudimentary” theory of mind (id.), so the ability to understand the thinking of others and how they arrived at their beliefs may be a uniquely human trait that develops by the end of early childhood.

I have a unique memory of a moment during childhood when I first became consciously aware of what I would know call “theory of mind”, refined my internal theory of it, and realized that an assumption I had made about it didn’t quite make sense.

When I was a young child in school, I started attempting to hum a tune for one of my teachers from one of the early stages of Sonic the Hedgehog. I expressed my disbelief when she stated that she had never heard this music before. However, then I thought about it, and realized the unconscious assumption (although I would not have known those words to refer to it) that had lead me to expect her to have heard of it. I had previously been using a heuristic that the longer I had known about something, the more likely it was for other people (most likely especially adults) to have heard of it. However, at this moment, I realized this, and thought to myself, “why would she have played that game? Why would I think she would have played that game?” I realized that there was no reason to be sure she would have played it, and that there was no reason she would know that music if she hadn’t.
